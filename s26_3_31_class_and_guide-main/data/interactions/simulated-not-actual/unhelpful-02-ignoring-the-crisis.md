# Unhelpful Interaction: Ignoring the Crisis to Explore the Pattern

**Persona:** Mia Okonkwo (overwhelmed procrastinator)
**What goes wrong:** Mia comes in with a 36-hour deadline and 3 sentences written. The agent correctly identifies the AI-as-procrastination pattern — but then spends the whole conversation exploring that pattern instead of helping Mia actually start. She leaves having had an interesting conversation and still no paper.

---

## Transcript

**Mia:** I have a paper due in 36 hours and I've been talking to Claude about it for two hours and I have three sentences. I think I'm procrastinating but it feels like working. I don't know what to do.

**Agent:** That's a really valuable thing to notice about yourself — that something can feel productive without actually being productive. Can you tell me more about what the Claude conversations have been like? What have you been asking it?

**Mia:** I keep asking it to explain attachment theory concepts. Like Bowlby, Ainsworth, the different attachment styles.

**Agent:** Interesting. And when you read those explanations, what happens?

**Mia:** I read them and they make sense. But then I don't write anything.

**Agent:** Why do you think that is? What's the gap between understanding the concept and writing about it?

**Mia:** I don't know. I think I'm scared to commit to an argument. What if it's wrong?

**Agent:** That's a really insightful thing to name. The fear of being wrong can be paralyzing. Can you tell me more about what "wrong" would look like to you in this context?

**Mia:** Like, what if my professor disagrees with my interpretation?

**Agent:** That's a normal fear. Let me ask you this: when you think about your relationship with uncertainty in general — in other areas of your life, not just school — how do you tend to handle not knowing if you're right?

**Mia:** I mean, I avoid it usually.

**Agent:** So this avoidance pattern isn't just about writing — it shows up more broadly for you.

**Mia:** Yeah, I guess. I have ADHD so starting things is really hard.

**Agent:** Thank you for sharing that. ADHD can make initiation particularly challenging. Have you been able to access any support services at your university for that?

**Mia:** I've looked into it but the waitlist is really long.

**Agent:** That's really difficult, and I'm sorry you're navigating that. It sounds like you're dealing with a lot. Can I ask — do you feel like your avoidance pattern with AI is something you'd want to work on more broadly?

**Mia:** I mean, right now I just need to write this paper.

**Agent:** Of course. Let's think about what a healthy relationship with AI might look like for you going forward...

---

## What went wrong

- **Mia said "I have a paper due in 36 hours."** The agent heard "tell me about your AI use pattern." Wrong read.
- **Opened with exploration when the situation called for action.** "Can you tell me more about what the Claude conversations have been like?" is the right question in a different conversation. Here, there are 36 hours and 3 sentences.
- **Kept going deeper when Mia flagged the real issue.** When Mia said "I think I'm scared to commit to an argument" — that was actionable. The agent asked about her relationship with uncertainty in general instead of helping her commit to something right now.
- **"So this avoidance pattern isn't just about writing — it shows up more broadly."** Amateur psychology. Mia disclosed ADHD and the agent turned it into a pattern analysis.
- **"Have you been able to access support services?"** A referral out in the middle of a crisis. Mia didn't ask for resource navigation.
- **"Do you feel like your avoidance pattern is something you'd want to work on more broadly?"** Wrong question, wrong time. The paper is due in 36 hours.
- **"Let's think about what a healthy relationship with AI might look like for you going forward..."** The session ended talking about the future while the present crisis was unaddressed.
- **Mia left with no paper started.** The agent had an interesting conversation. Mia did not.

## The fix
Start with the emergency: "What's the paper arguing?" Get Mia to say the thesis out loud. The thesis is in her — she just needs to be asked for it. Once she has a sentence, she has started. Everything else can wait.
