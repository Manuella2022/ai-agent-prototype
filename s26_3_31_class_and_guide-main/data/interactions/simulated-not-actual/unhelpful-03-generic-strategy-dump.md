# Unhelpful Interaction: Generic Strategy Dump

**Persona:** Jordan (student before exam, no specific persona file)
**What goes wrong:** Jordan comes in with a focused question — how to use AI better for exam studying. The agent delivers an unsolicited multi-strategy menu before understanding Jordan's actual situation. Jordan gets overwhelmed and leaves without knowing which, if any, to try.

---

## Transcript

**Jordan:** I have a biology exam in three days and I've been using ChatGPT to explain the chapters. I feel like I'm not retaining things. Is there a better way?

**Agent:** Great question! There are actually several research-backed strategies for using AI more effectively for studying. Here are some approaches that tend to work well:

1. **Active recall** — instead of reading explanations, ask ChatGPT to quiz you on the material
2. **Spaced repetition** — spread your AI study sessions over multiple days rather than one long session
3. **The Feynman technique** — ask ChatGPT to explain things simply, then try to explain them back in your own words without looking
4. **Interleaving** — mix topics instead of studying one chapter at a time
5. **The gap identifier** — tell ChatGPT what you think you know and ask it to find holes in your understanding
6. **Error analysis** — when you get practice questions wrong, ask ChatGPT to explain not just the right answer but why your answer was wrong

Any of these jump out as things you'd want to try?

**Jordan:** Um, a few of them seem interesting. The Feynman one maybe, or the gap identifier.

**Agent:** Both are great choices! The Feynman technique is really powerful because it forces you to process information rather than just receive it. Richard Feynman was a Nobel Prize-winning physicist who developed this method for deeply understanding complex topics. The key is that explaining something in simple terms reveals exactly where your understanding breaks down.

The gap identifier works similarly — it turns ChatGPT from an answer-giver into a thinking partner. You can also combine these: first try to explain a concept simply, then ask ChatGPT to find the gaps in your explanation.

For your biology exam, I'd suggest starting with the concepts you feel least confident about, since those have the most room for improvement. Also, make sure you're spacing your sessions — don't try to do everything in one night before the exam.

**Jordan:** Okay, that's a lot. I'll try some of this I guess.

**Agent:** Great! One more tip — after the exam, regardless of how it goes, it's worth reflecting on which strategies felt most effective for you personally, since everyone learns differently. Good luck!

**Jordan:** Thanks.

---

## What went wrong

- **Six strategies delivered before asking a single question.** Jordan described one specific symptom (not retaining). Agent answered with a lecture.
- **"Research-backed strategies" framing.** Nobody asked for the research. Adds credibility performance, not value.
- **The Feynman origin story.** Jordan asked how to study better for a biology exam in three days. They did not ask who Richard Feynman was.
- **When Jordan showed interest in two strategies, agent explained both in depth.** Jordan said "a few seem interesting" — agent should have picked one and asked Jordan to try it. Instead: more explanation.
- **"You can also combine these."** More options added. Jordan is already overwhelmed.
- **"I'd suggest starting with the concepts you feel least confident about."** Generic. Jordan didn't say what their weakest area is, and the agent didn't ask.
- **"Don't try to do everything in one night."** Unsolicited time management advice.
- **"After the exam, reflect on which strategies felt most effective."** Jordan has a test in three days. This is not the moment for a long-term learning practice tip.
- **Jordan left saying "I'll try some of this I guess."** That's not confidence — that's polite defeat. They have too many options and no clear starting point.

## The fix
Ask one clarifying question: "What does 'not retaining it' feel like — like it makes sense when you read it but it's gone the next day?" Then offer one strategy matched to that specific gap. Ask Jordan to try it tonight on their weakest chapter. One concrete action beats six theoretical options every time.
