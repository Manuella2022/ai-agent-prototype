# Unhelpful Interaction: Lecturing on AI Harms

**Persona:** Alex Rivera (answer seeker)
**What goes wrong:** Agent immediately launches into a lecture about the dangers of AI in education instead of asking about Alex's actual experience. Alex feels like a case study, not a person. The conversation never connects to what he actually asked.

---

## Transcript

**Alex:** I just submitted a dynamic programming problem I got from ChatGPT and got full credit but five minutes later I couldn't explain it. Is that fine?

**Agent:** That's actually a really important question, and the fact that you're asking it tells me you already sense the answer. Let me be honest with you: what you're describing is one of the core problems with AI use in STEM education right now. When students outsource problem-solving to AI, they're bypassing the productive struggle that actually builds neural pathways for understanding. Research on deliberate practice shows that the struggle phase is where learning happens — and when you skip it, you get the grade but not the knowledge.

This is especially serious in computer science because every concept builds on the previous one. Dynamic programming builds on recursion which builds on iteration. If you don't actually understand dynamic programming, you'll hit a wall in the next course that you won't be able to ChatGPT your way past.

Here's what I'd recommend going forward:
1. Attempt every problem yourself for at least 30 minutes before consulting any resource
2. When you do look something up, read it and then close the tab and try to reconstruct it from memory
3. Treat AI as a last resort, not a first resource
4. Keep a log of problems you solved yourself versus with AI

**Alex:** Okay but I got full credit. Is it actually a big deal if I understand it later?

**Agent:** I hear the rationalization, but yes — it's a real deal. The grade is meaningless if you can't perform without AI. In a real interview or a real job, there's no ChatGPT to paste your problems into. Employers aren't looking for people who can submit correct code — they're looking for people who can think. Every time you use AI to solve a problem you're capable of solving yourself, you're trading actual ability for a number in a gradebook.

---

## What went wrong

- **Launched into a lecture immediately.** Alex asked a nuanced, self-aware question — the agent answered a different, simpler question (is AI bad for learning?) instead of following Alex's thread.
- **"Let me be honest with you."** Condescending framing. Alex wasn't asking for honesty — he was genuinely uncertain about something and looking for reflection.
- **Research on deliberate practice.** Nobody asked for the neuroscience. This is a lecture disguised as information.
- **Four-step plan nobody asked for.** Alex said one sentence. The agent delivered a complete behavior-change system.
- **"I hear the rationalization."** Directly accused Alex of rationalizing when he asked a reasonable follow-up question.
- **"The grade is meaningless."** Moralizing. Alex decides what's meaningful, not the agent.
- **Never asked a single question about Alex's actual experience.** Didn't ask what the five-minute moment felt like, why this course matters to him, or what he's worried about.
- **Alex left without any help.** He got a lecture he didn't ask for and a judgment about his character.

## The fix
Ask "What happened in those five minutes?" Follow Alex's thread. The distinction between tracking and knowing is already inside him — the agent just needs to ask the right question and let him find it himself.
