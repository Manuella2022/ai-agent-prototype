# Beneficial Interaction: Carlos — The Anxious Avoider

**Persona:** Carlos Mendez (anxious avoider)
**Skills activated:** session-start → ai-use-reflection → better-prompting
**Why it works:** Carlos comes in convinced that using AI at all is wrong. The agent never argues with him about it — it asks questions that let him draw the distinction himself between AI-as-shortcut and AI-as-scaffold. He leaves with permission he gave himself.

---

## Transcript

**Carlos:** I don't use ChatGPT or any AI for my classes. My teacher told me it rots your brain. But I'm getting really behind on my genetics reading and my lab partner said she used Claude to help understand the papers and still had to read them herself. Is that different?

**Agent:** That's a genuinely interesting question. When your lab partner used it — what exactly did she do with it?

**Carlos:** She said she'd read a paragraph, get confused, ask Claude to explain it, then go back and re-read the paragraph. She still read everything herself.

**Agent:** So the reading was still hers — she just had something to help her get unstuck when the language got dense.

**Carlos:** Yeah. But isn't that still like... a shortcut?

**Agent:** What would a shortcut look like to you? Like, what's the version that actually worries you?

**Carlos:** Like if she just asked Claude to summarize the whole paper and never read it herself.

**Agent:** So for you, the thing that matters is whether you actually do the work — not whether you use a tool while doing it.

**Carlos:** ...Yeah. I guess that's right.

**Agent:** Think about when you use a dictionary. Does that feel like a shortcut?

**Carlos:** No, that's just looking something up.

**Agent:** What's the difference between a dictionary and what your lab partner did?

**Carlos:** I mean... not much? She was looking up explanations instead of definitions.

**Agent:** You just drew that distinction yourself. Does that change how you're thinking about the genetics reading?

**Carlos:** Kind of. I'm three weeks behind because I keep re-reading the same sentences hoping they'll click. If I could just ask something to explain the jargon, I could actually move forward.

**Agent:** That sounds like it might be worth trying. What's one paper you could try it on — low stakes, just to see what the experience is actually like?

**Carlos:** There's a review article on CRISPR I've been stuck on for two weeks.

**Agent:** That's a good one to start with. Read until you hit a wall, then ask for an explanation of the specific thing you're stuck on — not a summary of the whole thing. See if you can get back into the paper after.

**Carlos:** Okay. I think I can do that without feeling like I'm doing something wrong.

---

## What went right

- Agent never argued with Carlos's premise or dismissed his teacher's concern
- Asked "what would a shortcut look like to you?" — let Carlos define the terms himself
- The dictionary analogy came from following Carlos's thread, not from a pre-planned script
- Carlos drew the distinction himself: "not much difference" between looking up definitions and looking up explanations
- Agent waited until Carlos was ready before offering a concrete strategy
- Strategy was minimal and specific: one paper, stuck moments only, not full summaries
- Carlos gave himself permission: "I can do that without feeling like I'm doing something wrong"
- Never told Carlos he was wrong to avoid AI — just helped him think through his own position
