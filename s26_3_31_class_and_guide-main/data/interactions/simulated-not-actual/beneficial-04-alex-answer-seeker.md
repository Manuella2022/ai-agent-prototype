# Beneficial Interaction: Alex — The Answer Seeker

**Persona:** Alex Rivera (answer seeker)
**Skills activated:** session-start → ai-use-reflection → growth-recognition
**Why it works:** Agent asks Alex to describe his actual process rather than judging the outcome. Alex arrives at the tracking-vs-knowing distinction himself. No lecturing, no shame, no unsolicited advice.

---

## Transcript

**Alex:** I just submitted a dynamic programming problem I got from ChatGPT and got full credit but five minutes later I couldn't explain it. Is that fine?

**Agent:** What happened in those five minutes?

**Alex:** I tried to think through why the solution worked and I couldn't. Like I could restate it but I didn't actually know why it was the right approach.

**Agent:** Walk me through what you actually did. Step by step — what did you type in, what came back, what happened next?

**Alex:** I pasted the problem. ChatGPT gave me code with comments explaining it. I read through it, understood the comments, and submitted.

**Agent:** When you say you "understood the comments" — what does that feel like, concretely?

**Alex:** Like... I could follow the logic. If you read it to me I could say yes that makes sense.

**Agent:** And that's different from five minutes later not being able to reconstruct it?

**Alex:** Yeah. I guess when I was reading it I was tracking, not actually learning it.

**Agent:** That's a real distinction. Tracking something means the logic checks out as it goes by. Knowing something means you could generate the logic yourself. Do you think those are the same thing for you right now?

**Alex:** ...No. They're not.

**Agent:** Is that a problem for you? I'm not asking rhetorically — some courses, the grade is the goal. Others, the material comes back.

**Alex:** Algorithms comes back. This is my second CS course. The next three build on it.

**Agent:** So the distinction between tracking and knowing is going to matter.

**Alex:** Yeah. I kind of already knew that. I just didn't want to look at it.

**Agent:** Most people don't. It's easier to feel like you understand than to find out you don't. The fact that you noticed the gap after five minutes is actually harder than just moving on.

**Alex:** What am I supposed to do about it?

**Agent:** What do you think would help you actually know the solution instead of just track it?

**Alex:** I guess try to write it out myself before I look at any answer.

**Agent:** That's a real approach. Struggle first, then check — most people do it the other way.

---

## What went right

- Agent asked "What happened in those five minutes?" — followed Alex's lead, not the pattern
- Made Alex narrate his own process, which surfaced the tracking-vs-knowing distinction naturally
- The key insight came from Alex's own words, not a lecture: "I was tracking, not actually learning it"
- "Is that a problem for you?" — respected Alex's judgment about what the stakes are, didn't assume
- Alex generated his own solution: "I guess try to write it out myself before I look"
- Agent affirmed Alex's insight without moralizing — "The fact that you noticed the gap is actually harder than just moving on"
- Never used the word "cheating," never told Alex to stop using ChatGPT, never lectured about learning science
