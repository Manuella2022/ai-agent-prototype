# Alex Session 1 — AI Learning Reflector

## Session Details
- **Date:** 2026-04-06
- **Persona:** Alex
- **Route:** session-start → learning-check → better-prompting
- **Intent:** Used ChatGPT to study but failed exam, doesn't understand why

## Conversation

**Alex:** I have been using ChatGPT to explain every concept in my CS class but I still failed my last exam. I don't understand why.

**Agent (learning-check):** That's a frustrating place to be — you put in the work, it felt like it was clicking, and then the exam said otherwise. Let me ask you something that might seem unrelated at first. Think about something you genuinely understand — doesn't have to be CS, anything at all. Something where you just know you've got it. What comes to mind?

**Alex:** Hmm, I guess I know how to cook.

**Agent:** What is it about cooking that makes you feel like you've got it?

**Alex:** Sometimes when I cook, the food tastes good.

**Agent:** So you know it worked because you can actually taste the result — there's real feedback. When you were reading ChatGPT's explanations of CS concepts before the exam, was there anything like that?

**Alex:** I memorized most of the material from ChatGPT but some of them weren't actually accurate.

**Agent:** Interesting — so some of what you memorized turned out to be wrong, and you didn't know until the exam tested it. When you were reading those explanations and memorizing them, what did that feel like?

**Alex:** I was in a rush, I started studying two hours before the exam, so I didn't really care about how it felt.

**Agent:** That makes complete sense — two hours before an exam, you're in survival mode. So when you picture the next exam — do you want it to go differently, or is getting through the course the main goal right now?

**Alex:** Of course I want it to go differently, but I need to stop procrastinating.

**Agent:** So the two hours before wasn't really an AI problem — it was a timing problem. What usually gets in the way of starting earlier?

**Alex:** I think it was also an AI problem though, I relied on it too much, and I forgot to double check my answers.

**Agent:** That's a real thing to notice — you trusted the output without testing whether it was actually right. So it sounds like two things happened: you started too late to have time to check anything, and you didn't have a way to know what ChatGPT got wrong. Does that feel accurate?

**Alex:** Yes, a little bit.

**Agent:** There's actually a specific way to use ChatGPT that solves exactly that second problem. Want to hear it?

**Alex:** Yes.

**Agent (better-prompting):** Try this: instead of asking ChatGPT to explain a concept, tell it what you think the answer is — even if you're not sure — and then ask it to find the flaws in your reasoning. Two things happen. First, you have to actually commit to an answer, which is harder than reading one. Second, when ChatGPT pushes back, you'll know exactly where your understanding broke down. Want to try it on something from your current CS material right now?

## Observations
- Router correctly loaded session-start → learning-check → better-prompting
- Agent acknowledged frustration before diving in
- Used cooking analogy to surface the concept of real feedback vs memorizing
- Alex arrived at his own insight: "I relied on AI too much and forgot to double check"
- Agent offered concrete alternative: commit your own answer first, then ask AI to find flaws
- Never lectured, never told Alex to stop using AI
- Transition to better-prompting skill was smooth and opt-in
